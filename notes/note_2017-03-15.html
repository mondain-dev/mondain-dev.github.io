Neven Sesardić, “From Bad Logic to Bad Philosophy: the Case of Alain Badiou,” Rightly Considered, (10 March, 2017):

\[\begin{align} (\exists x)(Sx & \rightarrow \neg Sa), \label{ref1} \\ \forall xSx & \rightarrow \neg Sa, \label{ref2} \end{align}\]

and \((\ref{ref1}) \leftrightarrow (\ref{ref2})\).

Proof: To show that \((\ref{ref1}) \rightarrow (\ref{ref2})\), we show that \(\{(\ref{ref1}), \forall xSx \}\vdash \neg Sa \), then by deduction theorem we have \((\ref{ref1}) \rightarrow (\ref{ref2})\).

\[\begin{align} (\exists x)(Sx \rightarrow \neg Sa) &\ \ \ \ &\text{(assumption)} \nonumber \\ Sb \rightarrow \neg Sa & & \text{(existential elimination)} \label{ref4}\\ \forall xSx & & \text{(assumption)} \nonumber \\ Sb & & \text{(universal elimination)} \label{ref6} \\ \neg Sa & & \text{(\ref{ref4}, \ref{ref6}, modus ponens)} \nonumber \end{align}\]

\((\ref{ref2}) \rightarrow (\ref{ref1})\):

\[\begin{align} \forall xSx \rightarrow \neg Sa &\ \ \ \ & \text{(assumption)} \nonumber \\ Sa \rightarrow \neg\forall xSx & & \text{(modus tollens)} \nonumber \\ Sa \rightarrow \exists x \neg Sx & & \text{(negation of } \forall\text{)} \label{ref10} \\ \exists x \neg Sx \rightarrow \neg Sb & & \text{(existential elimination)} \label{ref11} \\ Sa \rightarrow \neg Sb & & \text{(\ref{ref10}, \ref{ref11}, hypothetical syllogism)} \nonumber \\ Sb \rightarrow \neg Sa & & \text{(modus tollens)} \nonumber \\ (\exists x)(Sx \rightarrow \neg Sa) & & \text{(existential introduction)} \nonumber \end{align}\]

Hence, \((\ref{ref1}) \leftrightarrow (\ref{ref2})\). \(\blacksquare\)

Remark: Since \((\ref{ref1}) \leftrightarrow (\ref{ref2}) \leftrightarrow \neg \forall xSx\) (see Sesardić), we have \( \{\neg \forall xSx\} \vdash (\exists x)(Sx \rightarrow \neg Sa) \) in which the term \(a\) is not mentioned in the assumption. It can then be shown by universal introduction that \( \{\neg \forall xSx \} \vdash (\forall y)(\exists x)(Sx \rightarrow \neg Sy) \). Hence

\[\begin{aligned}\neg \forall xSx &\rightarrow (\forall y)(\exists x)(Sx \rightarrow \neg Sy) & \text{(deduction theorem)}\\ (\ref{ref1}) &\rightarrow \neg \forall xSx & \\ (\ref{ref1}) &\rightarrow (\forall y)(\exists x)(Sx \rightarrow \neg Sy) & \end{aligned}\]

On the other hand, \((\ref{ref1})\) is an instance of \((\forall y)(\exists x)(Sx \rightarrow \neg Sy) \), so \( (\forall y)(\exists x)(Sx \rightarrow \neg Sy) \rightarrow (\ref{ref1}) \), and therefore \( (\ref{ref1}) \leftrightarrow (\forall y)(\exists x)(Sx \rightarrow \neg Sy) \), which shows that the constant \(a\) is immaterial, contrary to Badiou's claim that ‘the predicate \(S\) is altogether particular, and is not replaceable by any predicate whatsoever, no more than is the individual constant \(a\)’.
